## Overview

### The War Itself

In 431 BCE, the two great powers of the Greek world went to war with each other, and the world they had built together began to come apart. On one side stood Athens — imperial, commercial, democratic, and naval, the city that had produced the Parthenon and Sophocles and the idea that ordinary citizens could govern themselves. On the other stood Sparta — austere, militaristic, oligarchic, and land-based, the city that had turned its entire society into a weapon. Between them, they had beaten back the Persian Empire a generation earlier. Now they spent nearly three decades destroying each other.

The Peloponnesian War (431–404 BCE) engulfed virtually the entire Greek world. It was fought on land and sea, in Sicily and Thrace, in Athens and Sparta's allied cities across the Aegean. It ended with the catastrophic defeat of Athens, the dismantling of its democracy, and a Greek world so exhausted and fractured that it would fall, within a few generations, to the Macedonian kingdom of Philip II and his son Alexander. The war that was supposed to settle the question of Greek supremacy instead ensured that no Greek city would ever be supreme again.

### "A Possession for All Time"

Near the opening of his History, Thucydides draws a sharp and deliberate contrast between his own work and everything that had come before it. He dismisses the poets — Homer above all — for embellishing events to make them more pleasing than accurate. He dismisses the logographers, the early prose chroniclers, for writing to win an immediate audience rather than to record what actually happened. He even, with remarkable self-confidence, dismisses the pleasure of reading his own account as a secondary concern.
What he is offering instead, he says, is something harder and more durable:

"It will be enough for me if these words are judged useful by those who want to understand clearly the events which happened in the past and which (human nature being what it is) will, at some time or other and in much the same ways, be repeated in the future. My work is not a piece of writing designed to meet the taste of an immediate public, but was done to last forever."

This is an astonishing claim. Thucydides is not simply saying his account is more accurate than Homer's. He is proposing a new theory of what history is for. The past is not a storehouse of glorious deeds to be celebrated, or moral lessons handed down by the gods. It is a pattern — a recurring structure built into human nature itself — and if you study it with enough rigor and honesty, you will be equipped to understand the present and anticipate the future. History, in this view, is a form of political science.

### What Makes It Unlike Anything Before or Since

Before Thucydides, history as a discipline barely existed. Herodotus, his older contemporary, had written the first great prose account of the Persian Wars — and it is a magnificent, sprawling, digressive work, full of ethnography, legend, divine intervention, and wonder. Herodotus wanted to preserve the memory of great deeds and explain why things happened in the broadest possible terms, including the will of the gods and the patterns of fortune. He is endlessly curious, often credulous, always entertaining.

Thucydides reads like a different species of writer entirely. Several qualities set him apart not just from Herodotus but from virtually every historian who came after:

Radical exclusion of the supernatural. The gods do not act in Thucydides. Oracles appear, but only as things people believe in — and he is quietly clinical about the gap between religious expectation and actual outcome. The causes of events are always human: fear, honor, interest. This is so radical for its time that it is hard to overstate. He essentially invented secular historical explanation.

The speeches. Thucydides punctuates his narrative with long, formal speeches — the funeral oration of Pericles, the Melian Dialogue, the debate over Mytilene — which he acknowledges he could not have recorded verbatim. He says he tried to reproduce "the general sense of what was actually said," while keeping to "what was called for by each situation." The result is something neither pure document nor pure invention: a form of intellectual drama in which the arguments are real even if the words are reconstructed. The Melian Dialogue, in particular — in which Athenian envoys bluntly tell the island of Melos that "the strong do what they can and the weak suffer what they must" — remains one of the most unflinching analyses of power ever written.

Psychological and structural analysis. Thucydides is always asking why, but his whys are structural. He distinguishes between the immediate cause of the war (a dispute over Corcyra and Potidaea) and the truest cause (Spartan fear of growing Athenian power) — a distinction that political scientists still use to analyze conflicts today. He understood that nations, like people, can be driven to war by anxieties they barely articulate to themselves.

The unflinching moral register. Thucydides does not moralize — but the moral weight of what he describes is immense. He traces, in real time, the corruption of language itself during the civil war at Corcyra: how words like "courage" and "caution" and "loyalty" are systematically redefined to serve factional violence, until no shared standard of judgment remains. This passage reads less like ancient history than like a warning composed yesterday.

The incompleteness. The fact that the History simply stops — that we never get Thucydides's account of the Sicilian Expedition's aftermath, the revolution of 411, or the final Spartan victory — gives the text an eerie, open-ended quality that no finished work could have. It reads like a ruin, and ruins last.

### Why It Matters

Thucydides invented something that did not exist before him: the idea that the careful, critical, unsentimental study of past political and military conflict could yield knowledge — transferable, applicable knowledge — about how power works and how states behave. Every realist tradition in international relations theory, from Machiavelli to Hobbes to Kissinger, traces itself back to him. Generals study the Sicilian Expedition. Diplomats study the Melian Dialogue. Political philosophers study Pericles's funeral oration as the defining statement of democratic self-understanding.
He wrote, he said, for all time. Twenty-four centuries later, the argument is not yet over.

## The Author

Thucydides was an Athenian — a general, an aristocrat, and a man who lived through the very events he described. He contracted the plague that devastated Athens in 430 BCE and survived it. He was elected strategos (general) in 424 BCE, failed to prevent the fall of the city of Amphipolis to the Spartan commander Brasidas, and was exiled for it. That exile, which lasted twenty years, gave him something invaluable: the freedom to travel both sides of the war, to interview participants on every front, and to observe the conflict with the detachment that defeat forces upon a man.

He almost certainly did not live to finish his work. The *History* breaks off abruptly in 411 BCE, mid-sentence, mid-war, thirteen years before the end — a wound in the text that has fascinated readers ever since.

## Method and Innovation

### A New Kind of Intellectual Seriousness

Thucydides announces his method before he begins his history, and the announcement is itself an act of aggression. The opening sections of Book I — known to scholars as the Archaeology — are not scene-setting or invocation. They are a demolition. He works through the received traditions of Greek history and memory and shows, one by one, that they cannot be trusted: not because people lied, but because they did not think carefully about what it meant to know something. His project begins, in other words, with epistemology — with the question of how historical knowledge is even possible, and under what conditions it deserves belief.

This was genuinely new. Herodotus had been critical of sources, occasionally skeptical of legends, willing to report multiple versions of an event while withholding personal judgment. But Herodotus's skepticism was selective and his curiosity finally stronger than his doubt. Thucydides's skepticism is foundational. It is not a habit of mind but a constitutive principle: if you cannot say how you know, you do not know.

### The Rejection of Myth

The Greek word *mythos* did not originally mean "false story." It meant, roughly, "what is said" — tale, narrative, account. It shaded into what we call myth only gradually, as writers like Thucydides began to use it in contrast to logos (reasoned argument, factual account) to mark the distinction between what people believed and what could be demonstrated. Thucydides is one of the primary architects of that distinction.

His attack on myth operates on two fronts: poetic tradition and popular memory.

Against the poets — Homer most conspicuously, but also the entire tradition of epic and lyric — he makes a structural argument. Poets are not in the business of accuracy. They are in the business of pleasing audiences, and pleasure systematically distorts the past. Homer's account of the Trojan War has inflated the size of the expedition, because making it larger makes it more glorious, and glory is what his audience wants. Thucydides accepts that the Trojan War happened — that much seems recoverable — but reconstructs its probable scale from what can be reasoned about Bronze Age Greek logistics. The result is considerably less magnificent than the Iliad. Thucydides records this deflation without apology.

Against popular memory, he makes a different argument: that people are lazy about the past, accepting the first account they hear and ceasing to inquire further. He gives a famous example: most Athenians in his own time believed that Hipparchus, killed by the tyrannicides Harmodius and Aristogeiton in 514 BCE, had been the reigning tyrant of Athens. In fact, he was not — Hippias was the tyrant; Hipparchus was merely his brother. The tyrannicides had killed the wrong man, and Athenian democratic mythology had never bothered to get the story straight because the emotional and political function of the story (celebrating the birth of democracy) did not require accuracy. The past, Thucydides implies, is routinely held hostage to the present's need for useful fictions.

What distinguishes his rejection of myth from simple rationalism is that he does not merely dismiss what cannot be proven. He works with what remains. The *Archaeology* demonstrates a kind of inferential archaeology avant la lettre: he reasons from the poverty of early settlements (no great walls, no surplus wealth), from the instability of early populations (no fixed settlement, constant migration), from the logic of maritime power and trade, to construct a picture of early Greece that is modest, slow-developing, and credible precisely because it is undramatic. Absence of evidence is itself evidence; the scale of real events can be reconstructed from material conditions even when documents are lacking.

### Eyewitness and Inference: The Evidentiary Standard

Thucydides describes his evidentiary practice with a care that is almost painful in its self-awareness:

"As for the facts of the occurrences of the war, I have thought it my duty to give them, not as ascertained from any chance informant nor as seemed to me probable, but only after investigating with the greatest possible accuracy each detail, in the case both of the events in which I myself participated and of those reported by others."

Several things in this statement deserve unpacking.

Personal participation ranks highest. He was present at some events and makes the most of it — his account of the plague in Athens (430 BCE) is written with the clinical precision of a man who watched people die and survived himself, noting symptoms, progression, the failure of medicine, the collapse of religious observance, the social dissolution that followed. He does not speculate about divine cause. He describes.

Reported testimony is treated with explicit caution. He acknowledges that witnesses to the same event frequently disagree, "as they were partial to one side or the other, or as they remembered." This is a remarkably modern observation — the unreliability of eyewitness testimony is a standard concern of contemporary psychology, and Thucydides identified it in the fifth century BCE. His solution is not to abandon testimony but to cross-examine it: to seek multiple sources, to test accounts against each other and against probability, to distrust narratives that are too convenient.

Inference from probability fills the gaps. When evidence is unavailable, Thucydides reasons from what is humanly and logically likely given the circumstances. This is not speculation in the pejorative sense — it is disciplined reasoning from known premises about human behavior, political motivation, and strategic logic. He is, in this sense, doing something closer to social science than to archival history as we understand it: he is building a model of how states and individuals behave under pressure, and using that model to interpret the fragmentary record.

What he does not do — and this absence is itself a method — is appeal to divine will, fate, or any cause that cannot be traced to human decision and human character. The plague is not a punishment; it is a catastrophe with symptoms and a trajectory. The Athenian defeat in Sicily is not hubris punished by the gods; it is the result of specific strategic errors, institutional failures, and the reckless ambition of Alcibiades combined with the excessive caution of Nicias. The theological frame that structures Herodotus's world is simply absent from Thucydides, and its absence is total and deliberate.

### The Formal Speeches: The Central Methodological Problem

No aspect of Thucydides's method has generated more scholarly controversy than his use of speeches — and rightly so, because they represent the most productive tension in the entire text.

He includes roughly forty speeches, ranging from brief battlefield exhortations to extended deliberative debates. They include the *Funeral Oration of Pericles*, which is among the most celebrated pieces of political prose in Western literature; the *Mytilenean Debate*, in which the Athenian assembly first votes to massacre the entire male population of a rebellious ally and then, the next day, reverses itself; the *Melian Dialogue*, in which Athenian envoys and Melian representatives debate the terms of Melian surrender with merciless philosophical clarity; and *Pericles's last speech to the Athenian assembly*, in which he defends his strategy while Athens is devastated by plague and Spartan invasion simultaneously.

Thucydides addresses his method directly, in one of the most discussed sentences in historiography:

"As to the speeches that were made by different men, either when they were about to begin the war or when they were already engaged therein, it has been difficult to recall with strict accuracy the words actually spoken, both for me as regards that which I myself heard, and for those who from various other sources have brought me tidings of such speeches. As my opinion is that each man would have said what was especially called for in the given situation, I have accordingly caused them to speak what I thought would best express what the situation called for, though I have adhered as closely as possible to the general sense of what was really said."

This sentence has two halves that pull against each other. "I have adhered as closely as possible to the general sense of what was really said" — that is a commitment to accuracy. "I have caused them to speak what I thought would best express what the situation called for" — that is a license for invention. Scholars have argued for centuries about which half dominates, and the honest answer is that both are operative at once, which is exactly what makes the speeches so strange and so powerful.

Several things can be said with reasonable confidence:

Some speeches are probably close to what was actually said. Thucydides was personally present at some of these events. He had sources with access to others. The Athenian assembly was a public institution; debates were witnessed by thousands of citizens. Pericles's Funeral Oration was a public ceremony. These were not secret councils, and the general tenor of arguments made at them would have been widely known.

Some speeches are clearly intellectual reconstructions. The *Melian Dialogue* in particular reads like a philosophical dialogue, almost a Platonic elenchus in structure — claim and counter-claim, premise and inference, pressed to logical conclusions that real diplomatic negotiations rarely reach. No actual negotiation proceeds with quite this degree of argumentative tidiness. Thucydides has distilled the logic of imperial power relations into a form so pure it feels staged.

The speeches function as analytical instruments. By juxtaposing speeches — the Corinthians and the Athenians at Sparta before the war, the Mytilenean debate's Cleon and Diodotus, the Nicias and Alcibiades arguments over the Sicilian expedition — Thucydides forces his reader to do something a plain narrative never could: to think through competing strategic and moral arguments in real time, to weigh them, to find oneself, perhaps, persuaded by Cleon's brutal logic before being persuaded by Diodotus's cooler calculation. The speeches make the reader a participant in the decisions of the past, and therefore more equipped to recognize the same structures of argument when they appear in the present.

### What "Accuracy" Meant in Ancient Historiography

The Greek word Thucydides uses for his standard of accuracy is *akribeia* — precision, exactness, scrupulousness. It is the word used for a fine craftsman's work, for the precise measurement of a surveyor, for the careful reasoning of a mathematician. It carries a sense of effort against resistance: *akribeia* is what you achieve by working against the tendency of things to be vague, confused, and imprecise.

But *akribeia* in ancient historiography did not mean what "accuracy" means to a modern historian trained in archival methods. There were no archives in the modern sense. There were no systematic public records of debates or decisions. Documents existed — treaties were inscribed on stone, financial accounts were kept — but the granular documentary base that underlies modern historical scholarship simply did not exist for most of what Thucydides wanted to describe. When he says he achieved accuracy, he means something closer to: I have done everything a careful, honest, intelligent person could do to get as close to the truth as the available evidence permits.

This means his *akribeia* has two distinct registers that are worth separating:

Factual accuracy about events — troop numbers, battle outcomes, dates, locations — where he is genuinely trying to get things right and where he is, so far as we can verify him against archaeology and other sources, largely reliable, though not infallible. He sometimes compresses or simplifies military details. His chronology is occasionally difficult to reconcile with other evidence. But he is not inventing, and his errors are the errors of a careful man working under difficult conditions, not of a careless or dishonest one.

Analytical accuracy about causes and character — where his standard is not documentary verification but probability: what must have been true given what he knows about human nature, political motivation, and the logic of events. This is the register in which the speeches operate. When Pericles speaks in Thucydides, the question is not "did Pericles say these exact words?" but "does this speech correctly represent Pericles's strategic vision and the logic of Athenian imperialism?" Thucydides would say yes — and he would argue that this kind of correctness is more important than verbal transcription, because what matters is not the accidents of a particular formulation but the underlying argument.

This distinction — between surface accuracy and structural truth — is not a rationalization for making things up. It reflects a genuine and sophisticated view about what historical understanding consists of. Thucydides is not trying to produce a transcript. He is trying to produce understanding, and he believes that understanding requires getting at causes and arguments and patterns of behavior, not merely at surfaces.

### The Paradox at the Heart of the Method

There is a tension in Thucydides that he does not quite resolve, and that perhaps cannot be resolved. He insists on rigorous empiricism — reject what cannot be verified, distrust what is merely pleasing, prefer the difficult truth to the flattering legend. And then he places at the center of his work a series of speeches that he acknowledges are, at least in part, his own compositions.

The most charitable — and probably correct — interpretation is that Thucydides understood something that purely documentary historians sometimes miss: that the structure of an argument can be true even when the words recording it are reconstructed. The Athenians at Melos really did think and behave the way the *Melian Dialogue* suggests; the evidence of their actual conduct confirms it. Pericles really did hold the strategic vision that his speeches articulate; the coherence of Athenian strategy under his leadership demonstrates it. Cleon really was the kind of demagogue his speech at Mytilene reveals; his entire career in Thucydides is of a piece with it.

What Thucydides has done, in other words, is build a method that distinguishes between two kinds of accuracy — the accuracy of surface and the accuracy of structure — and argues, implicitly but insistently, that the second is the deeper and more important kind. The words can be reconstructed; the logic cannot be falsified. And it is the logic — of power, of fear, of democratic assembly, of imperial overreach — that he wants his reader to carry away.
That is why the History reads, uniquely among ancient texts, as though it could have been written last year: not because Thucydides was prophetic, but because he was precise about the things that do not change.

## Structure and Survival

### What We Have

The *History of the Peloponnesian War* comes down to us in eight books — a division that is almost certainly not Thucydides's own. Ancient texts were written on papyrus rolls, and the division into numbered books was typically the work of later editors and librarians, particularly the scholars of the great Library of Alexandria in the third and second centuries BCE, who standardized and catalogued the texts of classical antiquity. The eight-book structure we inherit is their imposition on a manuscript tradition, not an authorial design. Thucydides himself left no table of contents, no chapter headings, no explicit signal of where one unit of the work ends and another begins.

What he left was a continuous Greek prose manuscript — dense, syntactically complex, and in places clearly unrevised — that runs from the origins of the war in 431 BCE to a sentence in the autumn of 411 BCE, and then simply stops. No conclusion. No summing up. No gesture toward what is to come. The narrative ends in the middle of events, in the middle of a year, in what many scholars believe is the middle of a sentence, and the war it is describing still has seven years left to run.

This is not a rhetorical device. It is a death.

## Books

Despite the incompleteness, the eight books do have an internal logic — partly designed, partly visible only in retrospect — that rewards attention.

### Book I — The Archaeology and the Causes of the War

Book I is architecturally the most complex and intellectually the most ambitious. It is essentially two works in one. The first part — the Archaeology — is the methodological preface already discussed: a survey of early Greek history constructed by inference and probability, clearing the ground of myth and establishing Thucydides's evidentiary standards. The second part is a detailed account of the immediate origins of the war: the disputes over Corcyra and Potidaea, the debates at Sparta and Corinth, the decision for war. Between them, Thucydides inserts the Pentecontaetia — a compressed history of the fifty years between the Persian Wars and the outbreak of the Peloponnesian War — which functions as the essential backstory: how Athens built its empire, how Spartan anxiety grew, and why the collision was, in Thucydides's view, ultimately inevitable. Book I is thus simultaneously preface, methodology, backstory, and opening act. It is extraordinarily dense, and it repays re-reading more than any other part of the work, because everything that follows is implicitly foreshadowed in it.

### Book II — The First Years of the War

Books II and III cover the first years of the war, the so-called Archidamian War (named for the Spartan king Archidamus II), from 431 to roughly 428 BCE. This is where Thucydides's narrative hits its first great heights.

Book II opens with the Theban attack on Plataea, the formal declaration of hostilities, and then — with an abruptness that is itself a rhetorical gesture — the Funeral Oration of Pericles at the end of the war's first year, followed almost immediately in Book II by the plague that struck Athens in 430. The juxtaposition is devastating: Pericles has just delivered the most eloquent defense of Athenian civilization and democratic life ever recorded, and within pages the city is dying in the streets, its institutions collapsing, its dead unburied. Thucydides did not need to editorialize. The structure speaks.

### Book III — Mytilene and Plataea

Book III contains the Mytilenean Debate — the Athenian assembly's two-day argument over whether to massacre the population of a rebel ally — and the Corcyrean Revolution, Thucydides's analysis of civil war as a general political pathology. These are two of the most important passages in the entire work, and their placement in the same book, in quick succession, suggests deliberate thematic orchestration: both are about the corruption of judgment under pressure, the degradation of political language, the drift from principle to expediency that war accelerates.

### Book IV — Pylos and Amphipolis

Book IV and Book V cover the middle years and the Peace of Nicias (421 BCE), the uneasy armistice that ended the Archidamian War without resolving any of the underlying tensions. This section includes the famous Athenian victory at Pylos, the capture of Spartan soldiers at Sphacteria (an event Thucydides clearly regards as significant beyond its military weight — the inviolability of the Spartan warrior had been a cornerstone of Greek psychology, and these men surrendered), and the remarkable campaigns of Brasidas in Thrace, the Spartan general who nearly unraveled Athenian power in the north and whose death in battle — on the same day as Cleon's — ended the Archidamian War by removing its two most hawkish protagonists simultaneously. Thucydides records this coincidence without comment. He does not need to comment.

### Book V — The Peace of Nicias and the Melian Dialogue

Book V also contains the Melian Dialogue (416 BCE), positioned with careful deliberateness just before the narrative of the Sicilian Expedition. The Athenians force the neutral island of Melos into submission, execute its men, and enslave its women and children. The next book opens with the decision to invade Sicily. The placement is Thucydides's quiet argument: Athens has become what it claimed to oppose, and what follows is consequence.

### Book VI — The Sicilian Expedition

Books VI and VII are widely regarded as the greatest sustained narrative in ancient historiography, and among the greatest in any historiography. They tell the story of the Sicilian Expedition (415–413 BCE): Athens's catastrophic attempt to conquer Sicily, the largest naval expedition in Greek history, ending in the total destruction of the Athenian fleet and army in the harbor of Syracuse and on the retreat inland. Nearly fifty thousand men — soldiers, sailors, rowers, allies — were killed or enslaved. The Athenian navy, which was the foundation of Athenian imperial power, was effectively annihilated.

Thucydides tells this story with terrible control. He is not writing tragedy in the formal sense, but the Sicilian narrative has the structure of a tragedy: the initial hubris of the decision, the ominous mutilation of the Hermae on the eve of departure, the fatal duality of command between the reckless Alcibiades and the overcautious Nicias, the slow entrapment of the expedition, and the final catastrophe in the harbor — a scene described with such precision and such restrained horror that it remains one of the most powerful passages of historical prose ever written. Thucydides records that those watching from the shore could see the battle's fortunes shift by the changing expressions on the faces of their companions, that the watching army "fell into lamentations, groanings, cryings out" — and then he describes the retreat, and the dying, and the quarries of Syracuse where the Athenian prisoners were penned and left to perish.

### Book VII — The Destruction in Sicily

Book VII is where Thucydides's controlled, analytical prose comes closest to something that feels like grief. It covers the final destruction of the Athenian expedition in Sicily — the failed assaults on Syracuse, the arrival of the Spartan general Gylippus, the gradual entrapment of the Athenian fleet in the Great Harbor, and the last catastrophic naval battle in which Athens loses everything. What makes it unlike anything else in the *History* is the quality of the watching. Thucydides positions the Athenian land army on the shore as spectators of the sea battle that will decide their fate, unable to intervene, able only to observe — and he describes how their emotions shifted with the fortunes of the ships, how they groaned and cried out and fell into silence, how the watching itself became a kind of agony. Then the fleet is broken, and the army tries to retreat overland, and Thucydides follows them step by step as they dissolve — forty thousand men turned into a column of starving, wounded refugees, abandoned by their generals, cut down at a river crossing, penned into the stone quarries of Syracuse where most of them die in the heat and the filth. He renders this without lamentation, without any explicit statement that this is tragedy, without a single sentence telling the reader how to feel. The restraint is the devastation. By the time Book VII ends, Athens has lost the greatest military expedition in Greek history, and Thucydides has simply described it, which turns out to be the most annihilating thing he could have done.

### Book VIII — The Oligarchic Revolution

Book VIII is where the architecture fractures. It covers 411 BCE — the oligarchic revolution in Athens that temporarily overthrew the democracy, the naval campaigns in the Aegean, the complex triangular politics of Athens, Sparta, and Persia. It is noticeably different in texture from everything that precedes it. The speeches largely disappear. The narrative is more compressed, more documentary, sometimes harder to follow. There are signs of haste, or incompletion, or of a section still in draft that never received the polishing that Books II through VII clearly did. And then it ends, in the late autumn of 411 BCE, seven years before the war's conclusion.

#### Signs of Incompletion

The unevenness of Book VIII is not the only evidence that the History was unfinished. Scholars have identified several internal inconsistencies and apparent loose ends that suggest a work still in revision:
There are duplicate or inconsistent passages — places where Thucydides seems to have written two versions of the same event or explanation without having decided which to keep or how to reconcile them. The so-called "second preface" at the start of Book V, chapter 26 — in which Thucydides suddenly writes in the first person again, explains that he lived through the entire war and was able to observe it closely from both sides during his exile, and reasserts the importance of the work — reads less like a planned structural element than a note to himself, a reminder of where the larger argument was going, inserted mid-text and never quite integrated.
There are cross-references that point forward to discussions that never appear — passages where Thucydides says, in effect, "as I will explain later," and the later explanation is not there. Whether he intended to write it and ran out of time, or intended to go back and revise the forward reference, cannot be determined. The effect is of scaffolding left inside a building that was never quite completed.
The absence of a Sicilian aftermath is particularly striking. The destruction of the expedition is narrated in full; the news reaching Athens, the city's stunned incomprehension, the desperate rebuilding of the fleet — all of this Thucydides covers in Book VIII. But the longer consequences — the final decade of the war, the role of Persian gold, the Athenian defeat at Aegospotami in 405, the siege and fall of Athens in 404, the dismantling of the walls to the music of flutes — none of this is in Thucydides. We know it from other sources. Thucydides knew it too; his second preface tells us he lived until the war's end. He simply did not write it — or wrote it and the text was lost, which almost all scholars consider unlikely.

#### The Question of Continuators

Antiquity itself felt the incompleteness and produced two ancient attempts to complete the work. Xenophon's Hellenica begins almost exactly where Thucydides breaks off — in 411 BCE — and continues through the end of the war and beyond, into the fourth century. It is a capable work, sometimes vivid, but its limitations make Thucydides's greatness more visible by contrast: Xenophon is less analytical, more credulous, more conventionally pious, more inclined to let personal sympathies distort his judgments. Reading the Hellenica after Thucydides is like stepping out of a lighted room.
The Oxyrhynchus Historian — an unknown author known only from fragments discovered at the Egyptian site of Oxyrhynchus on papyrus in 1906 — also seems to have covered the period after 411 BCE with considerable competence, and some scholars have proposed that this anonymous writer understood the Thucydidean method better than Xenophon did. But the fragments are too incomplete for certainty.
Neither continuator is Thucydides. Neither could be.

#### The Break as Meaning

The incompleteness of the History is a fact about its physical survival — but it has become, over two and a half millennia of reading, something more than a fact. It has become a quality of the text.
Every careful reader of Thucydides eventually registers that the work breaks off before the catastrophe it was building toward. The fall of Athens, the end of the democracy, the Spartan victory that turned out to mean nothing lasting — all of this exists in the reader's knowledge but not in the text. We read Book VIII knowing what is coming. Thucydides does not tell us. The silence where the ending should be is filled, for every reader, with everything they know about how the story ends — and the knowledge that Thucydides himself lived through that ending, understood its significance, and died before he could write it, gives the break a weight that a completed ending could not have had.
There is also something philosophically appropriate about the incompleteness, though Thucydides did not plan it. His subject was a war that did not produce resolution — that exhausted both sides without truly deciding anything, that led not to stable victory but to continued fragmentation, the rise of Macedon, the eventual extinction of the independent polis. A history of such a war perhaps should not resolve. The abrupt stop in 411 BCE, with Athens still fighting, still capable of extraordinary resilience (the democracy was restored within a year of the oligarchic coup), still not yet finally defeated — this is, whatever its origin in authorial death, the right kind of ending for a work that was always more interested in process than in conclusion, in the ongoing logic of power rather than the finality of outcome.

#### Transmission and Survival

That we have the History at all is not obvious. Ancient texts survived through manuscript copying — hand-copied, generation after generation, through the tumult of late antiquity and the early medieval period, preserved primarily in Byzantine monasteries and scriptoria. Thucydides was never as widely read in antiquity as Herodotus or Livy; his Greek is notoriously difficult, his sentences long and knotted and sometimes genuinely obscure, his refusal of rhetorical ornament making him less useful as a model for orators and schoolboys. He survived partly by being considered important, partly by the accident of which manuscripts happened to be in which libraries when libraries were destroyed.
The earliest complete manuscripts of Thucydides date from the tenth and eleventh centuries CE — nearly fifteen hundred years after his death. A small number of papyrus fragments from Egypt push the textual tradition back somewhat further. From these manuscripts, and from quotations in other ancient authors, modern scholars have reconstructed a text that is generally reliable but contains genuine uncertainties — passages where the manuscripts diverge, where the Greek is so compressed as to be genuinely ambiguous, where editors must choose between readings that produce different meanings.
The eight-book structure, in other words, is the result of a survival process as much as an authorial design: what reached the Alexandrian editors, what those editors organized and numbered, what Byzantine monks copied, what Renaissance scholars rediscovered and printed. The History we read today is the precipitate of two and a half millennia of transmission, chance, and human effort — itself a kind of historical process that Thucydides, of all people, would have appreciated.

#### The Unfinished Monument

What survives is enough. It is more than enough — it is, by any measure, one of the handful of texts that permanently altered how human beings think about politics, power, and the past. The eight books we have contain the Funeral Oration and the plague, the Mytilenean Debate and the Melian Dialogue, the full terrible arc of the Sicilian Expedition. They contain Thucydides's entire method and his entire vision. What they do not contain — the final defeat, the silence after — we supply from our own knowledge of history, and the gap where the ending should be asks us to supply it actively, to think about what it means and why it happened, rather than accepting it from his hand already interpreted and arranged.
A finished History of the Peloponnesian War would have been a great book. The unfinished one is something stranger and more lasting: a work that keeps the reader inside the uncertainty of events still unresolved, a monument with an open wound at its center, a possession for all time that time itself had a hand in making.

## Key Themes

### Power and Fear: The Engine of History

Thucydides states his deepest thesis about power with unusual directness for a writer who normally prefers to show rather than tell. The truest cause of the Peloponnesian War, he says, was not the disputes over Corcyra or Potidaea — those were triggers, pretexts, the surface friction of two systems rubbing against each other. The truest cause was that Sparta was afraid. Athenian power had grown so rapidly, so visibly, and so seemingly without limit, that Sparta felt it had no choice but to fight while fighting was still possible. Fear — not greed, not ideology, not wounded honor, though all of those were present too — fear was the prime mover.

This is one of the most consequential analytical claims in the history of political thought, and it has never really been refuted. What Thucydides identified — the structural tendency of rising power to alarm established power, and of that alarm to generate conflict even when neither side consciously wants war — is now called the Thucydides Trap in contemporary international relations theory, invoked regularly in discussions of American anxiety about China, of NATO's relationship with Russia, of every configuration in which one state's growth feels to another like a zero-sum threat. The terminology is modern. The insight is twenty-four centuries old.

But Thucydides's treatment of power is more complex than a single structural thesis. He is interested in power not just as a geopolitical fact but as a psychological condition — in what it does to the people and states that possess it, and in the particular moral grammar that power generates and then corrupts. The Athenians at Melos articulate this grammar with disarming frankness: the strong do what they can, the weak suffer what they must, and any appeal to justice between unequal parties is simply a category error — justice is a concept that applies between equals, and Melos and Athens are not equals. This is not presented as Athenian villainy. It is presented as Athenian lucidity — as the clear-eyed acknowledgment of how interstate relations actually work once the decorative language of alliance and honor is stripped away.

What makes this scene so disturbing, and so enduringly useful, is that the Athenian logic is not wrong — and Thucydides does not say it is wrong. He simply shows it operating, and then shows what it costs. The Melians are killed and enslaved. The next book opens with Athens deciding to invade Sicily. The reader is left to make the connection that Thucydides declines to state: that a city which has fully internalized the logic of pure power, which has abandoned any internal restraint based on principle, has also disabled the self-corrective mechanisms that might warn it when its ambitions have exceeded its capacities. The same unsentimental realism that makes Athens effective makes it incapable of recognizing the limits of its own effectiveness. Power understood purely as domination is also, ultimately, a form of blindness.

### The Fragility of Democracy

Thucydides was an Athenian aristocrat who had been exiled by the Athenian democracy, and his relationship to democratic politics is one of the most subtly complicated things in the History. He is not a democrat in any simple sense. He is capable of remarkable admiration for democratic self-governance when it is working — the Funeral Oration of Pericles is one of the most beautiful defenses of democratic life ever written, and Thucydides gives it to Pericles without apparent irony. But he is also a diagnostician of democracy's structural vulnerabilities, and his diagnosis is devastating.

The core problem, as Thucydides sees it, is the relationship between democratic decision-making and long-term strategic coherence. Democracy depends on persuasion, and persuasion in a mass assembly is available to anyone with the skill to exercise it — including demagogues who understand crowds better than they understand strategy, who trade in fear and flattery, who win votes by telling the assembly what it wants to hear rather than what it needs to know. Pericles, in Thucydides's portrait, was the exception that proved the rule: a leader of sufficient stature, intelligence, and moral authority to lead the demos rather than follow it, to tell Athenians unwelcome truths and persuade them to accept restraint. The Funeral Oration belongs to a Athens still capable of that kind of leadership.

But Pericles dies of the plague in 429 BCE, and what follows is a study in democratic degeneration. The demagogue Cleon — whom Thucydides depicts with barely concealed contempt — wins the Mytilenean debate by appealing to Athenian anger and self-interest, nearly engineering a massacre. He wins at Pylos almost by accident and takes the credit with the Assembly. He dies at Amphipolis in a battle he should not have sought. After him comes Alcibiades — more brilliant than Cleon, more dangerous, a man whose individual genius and individual recklessness become indistinguishable from each other, who persuades the Athenian assembly to approve the Sicilian expedition with an intoxicating vision of empire and then is recalled to face trial before the expedition has even reached its objective, defecting to Sparta in the process and effectively destroying the mission he designed.

The democratic assembly that Pericles could lead becomes, after his death, an assembly that its leaders must flatter — and that eventually votes for the Sicilian Expedition not because it has been given an honest strategic assessment but because Alcibiades has made empire feel glorious and Nicias's cautious opposition has paradoxically strengthened the case for going, since the assembly interpreted his emphasis on the expedition's scale as an argument for sending more ships rather than fewer. This is a pathology specific to democratic deliberation: the assembly hears what it wants to hear, and skilled speakers learn to give it what it wants. The result is a city that loses the capacity to make accurate collective judgments about risk precisely when the stakes are highest.

Thucydides also traces the fragility of democratic institutions under the direct pressure of war and crisis. The oligarchic coup of 411 BCE — which Book VIII describes — was carried out by a relatively small group of conspirators who exploited the atmosphere of panic following the Sicilian disaster. Athens, which had seemed the invincible champion of democratic governance, dismantled its own democracy in a matter of weeks when frightened enough. Thucydides does not frame this as shocking. He frames it as predictable — as the kind of thing that happens when a political system is under sufficient stress and its citizens have been sufficiently frightened. Democracy, in this view, is not a natural equilibrium. It is an achievement, continuously maintained against internal and external pressures that are always working to undo it. Pericles's Athens was not democracy's natural state. It was democracy's best possible case, under ideal conditions, with an irreplaceable leader. The rest of the History is what happens when those conditions cease to obtain.

### The Corruption of Language: Stasis and the Corcyrean Revolution

The most philosophically radical passage in the History — and one of the most important pieces of political analysis ever written — is Thucydides's account of the civil war at Corcyra in Book III. Civil war (stasis in Greek — the word means literally a standing, a faction, but came to denote internal political violence) broke out across the Greek world during the Peloponnesian War, as the conflict between Athens and Sparta mapped onto pre-existing class tensions within each city: the democratic factions tended to align with Athens, the oligarchic factions with Sparta, and each sought outside support for its internal struggle. Corcyra was among the first and most violent of these civil wars, and Thucydides uses it as the occasion for a general analysis that goes far beyond Corcyra itself.
What he observes is not merely that people killed each other, or that atrocities were committed, or that normal standards of decency collapsed. All of that happened, and he reports it. What he focuses on — with an analytical precision that feels almost uncanny — is the corruption of language. Under the pressure of factional violence, words changed their meanings. The vocabulary of virtue was systematically reassigned to serve the vocabulary of power:

Reckless daring came to be regarded as courageous loyalty, cautious hesitation as specious cowardice, moderation as a cover for unmanliness, the ability to see all sides of a question as total inability to act.

This passage should be read slowly, because each item in the list describes something that is still happening. What Thucydides is describing is not merely the hypocrisy of calling bad things good. It is deeper and more frightening than that: it is the systematic inversion of evaluative language so that the words themselves no longer carry their original meanings, so that "courage" now means willingness to commit violence for your faction, "caution" means disloyalty, "moderation" means weakness. Once this inversion is complete, the community loses its shared vocabulary of judgment — the common language in which disagreements about conduct can be adjudicated, in which one side can appeal to the other on grounds both recognize. When words no longer mean what they mean, argument becomes impossible, and what is left is force.

This analysis has lost nothing in two and a half millennia. Every modern analyst of propaganda, authoritarianism, and political polarization is working in the territory Thucydides mapped. Hannah Arendt's analysis of totalitarian language, George Orwell's essays on political prose, contemporary scholarship on the degradation of public discourse — all of it is, in a sense, commentary on this passage in Book III. Thucydides understood that political communities are held together not just by shared interests or institutional arrangements but by shared language — by the common meanings attached to words like justice, loyalty, courage, and moderation — and that when faction and violence destroy those shared meanings, what is destroyed is the community's capacity to govern itself by reason rather than by terror.

He also notes something about the psychology of stasis that is particularly chilling: that in civil war, intelligence itself becomes a liability. The man who is capable of seeing complexity, who hesitates before acting because he understands that situations are complicated — this man is suspected by both sides. Factions reward certainty, decisiveness, and unconditional commitment. They punish nuance. The very qualities that make a person a good deliberator in peacetime make him a suspect in civil war, and so civil war systematically eliminates the people best equipped to end it.

### Hubris and Catastrophe: The Sicilian Pattern

The concept of hubris in Greek thought is often misunderstood as simply meaning arrogance or overconfidence. Its deeper meaning is the violation of proper limits — the transgression of the boundaries that separate what is yours from what belongs to others, or what is humanly possible from what belongs to the gods. In tragedy, hubris is typically punished by nemesis — the retributive force that restores violated order. It is a theological-moral framework, and Thucydides, as we have noted, was emphatically not a theological thinker.

And yet the structure of hubris-and-catastrophe is unmistakably present in the History — not as divine punishment but as political and strategic logic. Athens in Books VI and VII traces exactly the arc of tragic hubris, and Thucydides constructs that arc with the deliberateness of a dramatist, even as he refuses the theological framing that would make it explicitly tragic.

The Sicilian Expedition fails for specific, identifiable, human reasons: divided command, insufficient intelligence about the scale of Syracusan resistance, the recall of Alcibiades at the worst possible moment, the replacement of Lamachus (capable) with Nicias (overcautious and by the time of the expedition already dying of kidney disease), the failure to take Syracuse quickly before it could be reinforced, the Athenians' own delay when a naval escape was still possible. Each of these failures is particular, contingent, assignable to specific decisions by specific people. Thucydides traces them all. He does not say the gods brought Athens low.

But he has also constructed the narrative so that the expedition's deeper cause — its hubris in the classical sense — is visible beneath the particular failures. The decision to send the expedition was made with fundamentally inadequate knowledge of what Athens was taking on. Thucydides records that most Athenians did not know how large Sicily was, or that they would be fighting not just Syracuse but effectively the entire island. The decision was made in a state of collective fantasy, inflamed by Alcibiades's rhetoric and the traditional Athenian tendency to believe that what Athens decided to do, Athens could do. The same confidence that had built the empire — the willingness to act boldly, to trust Athenian ingenuity and naval superiority, to believe that risk could be mastered by intelligence — was, in Sicily, operating in a vacuum of actual information. Athens was not defeated by a superior enemy. It was defeated by its own image of itself.

This is what gives the Sicilian narrative its tragic structure even in the absence of divine machinery. The very qualities that made Athens great — its energy, its ambition, its democratic boldness, its willingness to attempt what others would not dare — were, taken to their limit, the qualities that destroyed it. Hubris in Thucydides is not a moral failing in the simple sense. It is what happens when virtues are not corrected by accurate self-knowledge, when confidence is not disciplined by genuine intelligence about the situation, when a community's image of itself becomes more powerful than its ability to see clearly. The expedition was Athens acting purely on Athenian self-belief, unrestrained by any institutional mechanism that could demand an accurate accounting of costs and risks. Nicias tried to provide one and was overridden. The result was catastrophe.

### The Interlocking of the Themes

What gives these themes their lasting power is that they do not operate separately in Thucydides. They are facets of a single analysis, each illuminating the others. Power generates fear, which generates conflict. The conflict of democracy with its own nature — its capacity for both extraordinary self-governance and extraordinary self-destruction — plays out within the conflict between states. The corruption of language is both a symptom and a cause of political disintegration: stasis corrupts language, and corrupted language makes stasis impossible to resolve. Hubris is what power looks like from the inside when it has lost the corrective of accurate self-knowledge — when a democracy can no longer deliberate honestly because its deliberative language has been captured by faction and flattery.

Thucydides does not offer a solution to any of this. He is not a moralist and he is not a reformer. He is a diagnostician, and his diagnosis is of the permanent, structural tendencies of political life under conditions of power and competition. He believed — his second preface says this explicitly — that human nature does not change, and therefore these patterns will recur. The value of studying them is not that the study will prevent recurrence. It is that the student of Thucydides, confronted with the early signs of these patterns in his own time, will at least be capable of recognizing what is happening — will not be deceived by the surface novelty of circumstances into missing the deep familiarity of the structure beneath.

That is the promise of the possession for all time: not comfort, not resolution, not the assurance that things will come right. Just clarity — the hard, unsentimental clarity of a man who watched his world destroy itself and understood, with unflinching precision, exactly how it was done.

## Selected Excerpts

### The Funeral Oration (Book II, 35–46)

Delivered by Pericles at the end of the war's first year, over the bodies of the Athenian dead, the Funeral Oration is the most celebrated piece of political prose in the ancient world, and one of the most contested. It is the high-water mark of the History — the moment at which Athens is most fully itself, before the plague, before the demagogues, before Sicily — and Thucydides places it with the precision of a tragedian, knowing that everything which follows will be its undoing.

The speech is structured as a sequence of concentric claims, moving from the outward to the inward. Pericles begins not with the dead but with Athens itself — with its constitution, its institutions, its way of life. Democracy, he argues, is not merely a form of government but a form of civilization: one in which political participation is open to all citizens regardless of class, in which private life is free from interference, in which the city is beautiful and its pleasures available to everyone, in which openness rather than exclusion is the operative principle. Athens does not expel foreigners or guard its military secrets; it trusts its own character. "We are a model to others," Pericles says, "not imitators of anyone else."

What is remarkable about this section is not just its content but its implied argument about the relationship between democratic culture and military power. Sparta's strength, everyone knew, came from total subordination of individual life to military training, from the suppression of pleasure, beauty, and private freedom in favor of collective discipline. The Spartan system produced formidable soldiers by making the entire city a barracks. Pericles argues — and this is the speech's most audacious claim — that Athens produces equally formidable soldiers without those sacrifices, that free men fighting for a civilization they love and inhabit are as effective as trained automatons, that the Athenian way of life is not a weakness that must be compensated for by military discipline but a source of genuine strength. Courage rooted in choice and understanding is more durable than courage rooted in compulsion.

The speech ends by turning to the dead themselves, and here Thucydides gives Pericles something unexpected: not consolation but a kind of severe clarification. To the parents of the fallen he offers not comfort but the suggestion that they have more children; to the young men present he offers not safety but the invitation to desire what Athens represents badly enough to die for it. It is the least sentimental funeral oration imaginable — which is precisely why it lands so hard. Pericles is not telling the mourners that their grief is bearable. He is telling them that their dead understood what they were dying for, and that this understanding was itself the achievement Athens represents.

The critical reader — and Thucydides is always educating a critical reader — notices that the speech also contains the seeds of what will destroy Athens. The confidence is real but also totalizing; the self-regard, however justified in 431 BCE, is already shading into the self-belief that will authorize Sicily. Pericles's Athens is genuinely magnificent. It is also genuinely incapable of imagining its own failure. The Funeral Oration is beautiful the way a perfect summer afternoon is beautiful — fully itself, unaware that it is about to end.

### The Plague (Book II, 47–54)

Within pages of the Funeral Oration, the plague arrives. Thucydides's juxtaposition is so deliberate and so devastating that it barely requires comment — except that it is not, in his framing, a divine judgment on Athenian pride. It is not a judgment at all. It simply happens, with the indifference of biological reality to human significance, immediately after the most eloquent defense of human civilization in the ancient world.

Thucydides survived the plague himself and says so, explaining that personal experience gave him more accurate knowledge of its symptoms than secondhand report could provide. The passage that follows is one of the earliest pieces of clinical medical description in Western literature — systematic, sequential, observational, stripped of theological explanation. He describes the onset in the head, the inflammation of the eyes, the descent into the throat and lungs, the violent coughing, the unquenchable thirst that drove patients into water cisterns, the reddening of the skin, the characteristic posture of the dying. He is doing what a good physician does: recording what can be observed in the hope that accurate description will be useful to those who encounter the disease later.

But the plague narrative does something no purely medical description does. Thucydides traces the social and moral dissolution that followed the biological catastrophe, and what he shows is that the fabric of Athenian civilization — the very civilization Pericles had just described as the highest achievement of human organization — unraveled with remarkable speed under sufficient biological pressure. The sick were abandoned by their caregivers, who then sickened themselves. The laws of burial collapsed; bodies were stacked on pyres that had been built for others. Religious observance failed — the temples filled with corpses, the prayers went unanswered, and the Athenians drew the logical conclusion that piety and impiety were equally useless and ceased to observe the difference. And most consequentially, those who survived the plague and knew they were now immune engaged in reckless pursuit of immediate pleasure, since they had learned that long-term plans and moral scruples offered no protection against dying in agony within a week.

What Thucydides is demonstrating — and this is the passage's larger argument, placed here with full intent — is the contingency of civilization. All of the institutions, norms, legal structures, and shared moral commitments that Pericles celebrated in the Funeral Oration rest on conditions that can be removed. They are not natural; they are constructed, maintained by habit, social pressure, and the reasonable expectation that the future will resemble the past. When a catastrophe destroys that expectation — when people stop believing that acting well now will produce good outcomes later, because the future has become radically unpredictable — the entire normative structure of civilized life can collapse within a single season. The Funeral Oration described what Athens was. The plague describes how fragile it was. Together they form the first great argument in Western thought about the relationship between civilizational achievement and civilizational fragility.

### The Melian Dialogue (Book V, 84–116)

In 416 BCE, fifteen years into the war, an Athenian fleet arrived at the small island of Melos — a Spartan colony that had refused to join the Athenian empire and tried to remain neutral. The Athenians sent envoys to negotiate before attacking, and Thucydides renders what followed as a formal dialogue: claim and counter-claim, argument and refutation, compressed to its logical skeleton with a philosophical rigor that no actual negotiation could quite have achieved. It is the most purely intellectual passage in the History, and the most morally harrowing.

The Athenian position is stated with a frankness that amounts to philosophical provocation: they have not come to make arguments about justice, because justice only operates between equals, and Athens and Melos are not equals. They have come to discuss practical reality. The Melians can submit and survive, or resist and be destroyed. The Athenians are quite explicit that their motive for demanding submission is not particular hatred of Melos but the logic of imperial maintenance: allowing a small island to remain independent by invoking its neutrality would suggest to other subjects that defiance is survivable, and that suggestion cannot be permitted. Melos must be destroyed not because Melos has done anything to Athens but because empire, once established, generates its own logical imperatives.

The Melians resist this logic with arguments from justice, from hope, and from the gods. They suggest that Athens, by acting unjustly, is creating precedents that will eventually be used against itself; that Sparta will come to their aid; that fortune is unpredictable and the gods favor the righteous. The Athenians dismiss each argument in turn, not cruelly but clinically. The gods, they suggest, favor power, since the consistent behavior of the divine order seems to operate on the same principle as the human one. Sparta will not help Melos because it is not in Sparta's interest to do so. Hope is a dangerous counselor — it is the resource of those who have no others, and it encourages people to ruin themselves waiting for salvation that does not come.

The dialogue ends. The Melians refuse to submit. The Athenians besiege the island, capture it, execute the men, and enslave the women and children. Thucydides records this in two sentences.

The passage's power comes from what Thucydides does not do. He does not condemn the Athenians. He does not vindicate the Melians. He does not invoke divine retribution, or note that Athens would itself be destroyed eight years later, or suggest that the Athenian logic was wrong in its own terms. He simply presents the argument at full force and the outcome at full starkness, and leaves the reader holding both. The reader who finds the Athenian argument compelling — and it is genuinely compelling, as pure analysis of how power operates — must also sit with the image of a small island's entire adult male population killed on its logic. The reader who finds the Athenian argument monstrous must reckon with the fact that Thucydides has not given the Melian counter-arguments anything like equal force. He is not adjudicating. He is describing. And the description is, among other things, a test of the reader's own moral and political commitments — of what you actually believe about the relationship between power and justice when the decorative language is stripped away and the choice is presented in its naked form.

The Melian Dialogue's placement immediately before the Sicilian Expedition is the History's most sustained piece of structural argument. Athens has just demonstrated that it operates purely on the logic of power, with no internal principle of restraint. Sicily is what that logic produces when it meets a situation it cannot control.

### The Retreat from Syracuse (Book VII, 75–87)

The Athenian army retreats from Syracuse beginning in the autumn of 413 BCE, and Thucydides's account of what follows is the most sustained piece of tragic narrative in ancient prose. It runs for thirteen chapters, and its method is the opposite of the Melian Dialogue's philosophical compression: it is granular, sequential, and almost unbearably present-tense in its effect, moving step by step with forty thousand men as they dissolve from an army into a column of refugees and then into scattered individuals and finally into bodies.

The retreat begins in collective shame. The expedition has lost its fleet in the Great Harbor; the survivors on land cannot look at the wreckage in the water without confronting what it means. Thucydides notes that they are abandoning their own unburied dead — a violation of religious duty so profound that it marks, for every Greek reader, the point at which normal human and religious life has ceased to be available to them. They are already outside the boundaries of the world they came from.

What follows is a march of attrition. The Syracusans and their allies harry the column constantly, cutting off stragglers, blocking water sources, turning every river crossing into a slaughter. Thucydides follows the two divisions of the army — one under Nicias, one under Demosthenes — as they gradually lose cohesion. Demosthenes's division is surrounded and forced to surrender; he and his men sit down in an olive grove and wait for death or capture. Nicias's division reaches the river Assinarus and what Thucydides describes is as close to pure horror as his controlled prose ever comes: men throwing themselves into the water out of exhaustion and thirst, drinking from a river that is already running with blood from the men dying upstream, trampling each other, the enemy cutting them down from the banks. Nicias, who has been dying of kidney disease throughout the expedition and who knows he carries the principal moral responsibility for the disaster, surrenders to the Spartan Gylippus personally, asking only that his men be spared. They are not spared.

The prisoners are penned in the stone quarries of Syracuse — open pits in the rock — in the heat of the Sicilian autumn, without adequate food or water, without shelter from the sun or the cold nights, surrounded by the smell of the dead. They die there over weeks, most of them. Nicias and Demosthenes are executed. The greatest military expedition Athens ever assembled ends in a pit in Sicily.

What Thucydides does with this material is perhaps the most technically impressive thing in the History. He maintains throughout what can only be called a compassionate detachment — he is not cold, but he is not weeping either. He records suffering with the same precision he brings to tactical analysis. The reader weeps, if the reader weeps, entirely on their own. Thucydides does not invite it and does not perform it. This restraint, which some readers have found inhuman, is in fact the deepest form of respect the historian can pay to catastrophe — the refusal to aestheticize it, to make it beautiful or morally satisfying, to offer the reader the release of authorial emotion as a substitute for genuine reckoning. He makes you look at it without providing the comfort of his own reaction to look at instead.

The retreat from Syracuse is also, structurally, the moment at which Thucydides's own thesis is most fully demonstrated. This is what happens when the logic of imperial power, uncorrected by accurate self-knowledge, unchecked by honest deliberation, inflamed by democratic fantasy and demagogic flattery, extends itself beyond what reality can sustain. The Funeral Oration was Athens at its peak of self-understanding. The quarries of Syracuse are Athens at the bottom of its self-delusion. Between them runs the entire argument of the History — not stated, not moralized, just shown, with the terrible patience of a man who watched his world destroy itself and thought it important enough to describe exactly right.

## Further Reading: Thucydides and the Peloponnesian War

### The Greek Text

The standard critical edition of the Greek text remains **S. C. Hobbs and H. Stuart Jones, *Thucydidis Historiae*, 2 vols. (Oxford Classical Texts, 1898–1901; revised 1942)**. It has been the scholarly baseline for over a century and, despite subsequent papyrological discoveries and textual debate, has not been superseded as the working text for most academic purposes. Those reading in Greek will also want to consult **A. W. Gomme, A. Andrewes, and K. J. Dover, *A Historical Commentary on Thucydides*, 5 vols. (Oxford, 1945–1981)**, which remains the most exhaustive line-by-line commentary in English, indispensable for anyone working closely with the Greek and essential even for those who are not.

### Translations

The history of Thucydides in English translation is itself an intellectual history of how each era wanted to read him.

**Thomas Hobbes (1629)** — the earliest major English translation, made by the philosopher before he had written anything of his own philosophical importance, is still worth reading. Hobbes chose Thucydides deliberately, as a thinker whose view of human nature confirmed and in some ways formed his own, and his translation has a knotted, muscular quality that conveys the difficulty of the Greek in a way smoother versions do not. It is not reliable as a scholarly text — Hobbes's Greek was capable but not expert, and the seventeenth-century English creates its own distance — but as an encounter between two of the most unsentimental minds in Western political thought, it is irreplaceable.

**Richard Crawley (1874; revised and reissued in various editions including Everyman's Library)** was for most of the twentieth century the standard English version, and it remains widely read. Crawley produces fluent, dignified Victorian prose that renders the speeches with real grandeur — his Funeral Oration is genuinely moving — but he sometimes smooths over the Greek's notorious syntactic difficulty and occasionally expands or clarifies where the original is compressed and obscure. He is the best starting point for a reader who wants the narrative to move.

**Rex Warner (Penguin Classics, 1954; revised 1972)** brought Thucydides to a mass readership and is probably still the most widely read version. Warner's prose is lucid and modern, occasionally too modernizing — he domesticates some of Thucydides's strangest constructions into standard journalistic English — but his translation of the Melian Dialogue is particularly strong, and for a first encounter with the *History* his version remains thoroughly serviceable.

**Steven Lattimore (Hackett, 1998)** is now the preferred scholarly translation for classroom and academic use. Lattimore takes the difficulty of the Greek seriously — he does not smooth over ambiguities but tries to reproduce them in English, even at the cost of some readability — and his introduction and notes are genuinely helpful. For anyone who wants to engage seriously with what Thucydides is actually saying, rather than a readable approximation of it, this is the edition to use.

**Peter Green (University of California Press, 2021)** is the most recent major translation, by a classical scholar who is also an accomplished prose stylist. Green argues strenuously in his introduction for a more confrontational approach to Thucydides's famous difficulty, resisting the temptation to clarify what is genuinely unclear in the Greek. The result is sometimes demanding but consistently stimulating, and his introductory essays are among the best short orientation to Thucydides in the language.

For the speeches in particular — where the gap between translations is widest and the stakes are highest — it is worth reading two or three versions side by side. The Funeral Oration, the Melian Dialogue, and Pericles's last speech reward this comparative treatment: the differences between how Crawley, Warner, Lattimore, and Green handle the same passages reveal as much about Thucydides as any single version can.

### Introductory and General Studies

**Jacqueline de Romilly, *Thucydides and Athenian Imperialism* (1947; English translation 1963)** is the foundational modern study of Thucydides's political thought, written by the scholar who did more than anyone in the twentieth century to establish him as a serious political and philosophical thinker rather than merely a military historian. De Romilly's analysis of how Thucydides traces the development and self-corruption of Athenian imperial ideology remains essential. Her later **'Thucydides' (1985)** is a more accessible single-volume introduction.

**W. Robert Connor, *Thucydides* (Princeton, 1984)** transformed scholarship on the *History* by insisting on reading it as a literary and rhetorical construction rather than primarily as a historical source. Connor pays sustained attention to structure, juxtaposition, and narrative technique — to the way Thucydides arranges his material to produce meaning beyond what the individual passages explicitly state. His reading of the Sicilian narrative as tragic structure is particularly influential.

**Simon Hornblower, *A Commentary on Thucydides*, 3 vols. (Oxford, 1991–2008)** is the modern scholarly commentary that has partially supplemented Gomme-Andrewes-Dover for the first four books (Hornblower's volumes cover Books I–V.24 and V.25–VIII). It is comprehensive, philologically rigorous, and essential for serious academic work, though not designed for non-specialists.

**Donald Kagan, *The Peloponnesian War* (Viking, 2003)** is the accessible one-volume condensation of Kagan's monumental four-volume history of the war (published by Cornell University Press between 1969 and 1987). Kagan is consistently willing to disagree with Thucydides's interpretations — he is more sympathetic to Nicias and more skeptical of Pericles than Thucydides — and the disagreements are productive. The four-volume series (*The Outbreak of the Peloponnesian War*, *The Archidamian War*, *The Peace of Nicias and the Sicilian Expedition*, and *The Fall of the Athenian Empire*) remains the most thorough modern historical treatment of the war itself.

**Victor Davis Hanson, *A War Like No Other* (Random House, 2005)** approaches the Peloponnesian War thematically — cavalry, infantry, plague, walls, naval warfare — rather than chronologically, drawing on Thucydides but also on archaeology and comparative military history. It is vivid and accessible, particularly strong on the material and physical realities of ancient Greek warfare, and useful for a reader who wants context for the military narrative.

---

### Thucydides's Method and Historiography

**F. M. Cornford, *Thucydides Mythistoricus* (1907)** is an eccentric and now largely rejected argument that Thucydides was more indebted to tragic and mythological patterns of thought than his rationalist self-presentation suggests. It is wrong in its central thesis but right in ways that subsequent scholarship took decades to recognize, and reading it alongside Connor's literary study is instructive.

**John Finley, *Thucydides* (Harvard, 1942)** remains a classic treatment of Thucydides's style, intellectual formation, and relationship to the sophistic movement and to fifth-century scientific thought. Finley's discussion of the connection between Thucydidean prose style and Hippocratic medical writing is particularly illuminating.

**Rosalind Thomas, *Herodotus in Context* (Cambridge, 2000)** is primarily about Herodotus but is essential reading for understanding what Thucydides was reacting against and transforming. By situating Herodotus in the intellectual culture of fifth-century Athens — the sophists, the physicians, the natural philosophers — Thomas makes the contrast between the two historians more precise and more interesting than the simple opposition of credulous storyteller and rigorous scientist usually suggests.

**Nino Luraghi, ed., *The Historian's Craft in the Age of Herodotus* (Oxford, 2001)** is a collection of essays examining Greek historiography in its broadest context, with several contributions directly relevant to Thucydides's methods and sources.

---

### Political Theory and Reception

**Graham Allison, *Destined for War: Can America and China Escape Thucydides's Trap?* (Houghton Mifflin Harcourt, 2017)** is the most influential recent application of Thucydidean analysis to contemporary geopolitics. Allison's central concept — the Thucydides Trap, the structural tendency of rising powers to generate conflict with established ones — has entered mainstream policy discourse. The book is uneven as political science but valuable as an illustration of why Thucydides's structural analysis of power retains its explanatory force.

**Leo Strauss, *The City and Man* (1964)** contains a long chapter on Thucydides that is among the most philosophically demanding readings ever written. Strauss argues that Thucydides has a hidden political teaching accessible only through careful attention to the work's silences and structural choices, and that this teaching is deeply skeptical of both democracy and imperialism. Whether or not one accepts Strauss's interpretive method, his reading forces engagement with questions about Thucydides's own political commitments that more straightforwardly historical approaches tend to avoid.

**Michael Doyle, *Ways of War and Peace* (Norton, 1997)** situates Thucydides within the history of realist international relations theory, offering the most rigorous academic treatment of his place in the tradition that runs through Machiavelli, Hobbes, and into contemporary IR scholarship.

**Edith Foster and Donald Lateiner, eds., *Thucydides and Herodotus* (Oxford, 2012)** collects essays comparing the two great historians across a range of methodological and thematic concerns, and is useful for anyone who wants to think carefully about what Thucydides inherited, what he rejected, and what he invented.

### On the Speeches

The literature on the Thucydidean speeches is enormous; the following are entry points rather than an exhaustive survey.

**H. D. Westlake, *Individuals in Thucydides* (Cambridge, 1968)** examines how Thucydides constructs individual political and military figures, with sustained attention to how the speeches contribute to characterization.

**Cynthia Farrar, *The Origins of Democratic Thinking* (Cambridge, 1988)** situates the Funeral Oration and Thucydides's democratic analysis in the broader context of fifth-century Athenian political thought, with important discussions of the relationship between Thucydides, Protagoras, and democratic theory.

**Nicole Loraux, *The Invention of Athens: The Funeral Oration in the Athenian Democratic Tradition* (Harvard, 1986)** is the definitive study of the Athenian funeral oration (*epitaphios logos*) as a genre and institution, locating Pericles's speech in Thucydides within a tradition of public self-representation that reveals as much about Athenian ideology as it does about any individual speaker.

---

### The Sicilian Expedition

**Peter Krentz and Everett Wheeler, eds.** aside, the single best treatment of the Sicilian Expedition as a historical event remains **Kagan's third volume** (*The Peace of Nicias and the Sicilian Expedition*). For its literary and structural significance within the *History*, Connor's *Thucydides* is again the essential guide. For the archaeology of Syracuse and the physical reality of the siege, **Brian Caven, *Dionysius I: War-Lord of Sicily* (1990)** provides useful context, and **A. W. Pickard-Cambridge's** older studies of Athenian military history remain valuable background.

### A Note on Reading Order

For a reader coming to Thucydides for the first time, the following sequence tends to work well: begin with a readable translation of the *History* itself — Warner or Crawley for narrative pleasure, Lattimore for precision — reading at minimum Books I, II (through the plague), III (the Mytilenean Debate and Corcyrean stasis), V (the Melian Dialogue), and Books VI and VII in full. Then read Kagan's one-volume history for the broader military and political context. Then return to Thucydides with Connor's *Thucydides* as a guide to what the literary and structural construction of the work is doing. De Romilly's study of imperialism and Allison's *Destined for War* can then provide the political-theoretical dimension. The commentaries — Gomme-Andrewes-Dover, Hornblower — are for sustained scholarly engagement and need not be read consecutively; they are reference works more than reading experiences.

The *History* rewards multiple readings at different levels and at different points in a reader's life. It is one of those texts that changes not because the text changes but because the reader does — because experience of politics, institutions, and human failure makes passages that seemed merely analytical feel, on rereading, like precise descriptions of something you have personally witnessed. That quality of recognition across twenty-four centuries is, finally, what Thucydides meant by a possession for all time.
