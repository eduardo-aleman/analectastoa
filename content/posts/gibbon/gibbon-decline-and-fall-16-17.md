### The Conduct of the Roman Government towards the Christians

#### The Motives of the Emperors

The primitive Christians derived the name and the sentiments of a nation who, maintained in the midst of a polytheistic world, a pure and uncompromising monotheism. The curiosity of the Roman princes was seldom directed toward the humble and obscure sectaries; yet the progress of this new religion gradually challenged the religious harmony of the ancient world. Gibbon suggests that the persecutions, often exaggerated by ecclesiastical historians, were frequently the result of political necessity rather than religious bigotry.[^1]

#### The Social and Political Transformation under Constantine

The foundation of Constantinople and the legal recognition of the Christian church marked a revolution more extraordinary than the conquest of provinces. By shifting the seat of empire to the East and embracing a hierarchical church, the Roman state underwent a metamorphosis that secured its survival in a new form, even as it sacrificed the ancient spirit of the Republic.

[^1]: In Chapter XVI, Gibbon famously interrogates the historical accuracy of martyrologies, applying a skeptical Enlightenment lens to the traditional narratives of the early Church's suffering.
